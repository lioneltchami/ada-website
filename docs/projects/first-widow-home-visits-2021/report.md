## 1. Executive Summary

In November 2021, the Apoti Development Association (ADA) carried out its inaugural community outreach project — a series of door-to-door home visits to elderly widows in Buea, South-West Region, Cameroon. A team of three female university-age volunteers identified and visited three elderly widows living in conditions of extreme vulnerability, delivering bags of rice, offering prayer and emotional support, assessing living conditions, and documenting urgent needs. This modest but deeply meaningful project marked the official launch of ADA as a faith-based organization committed to serving vulnerable communities, and the findings from these visits laid the groundwork for all subsequent programming.

---

## 2. Project Background

### 2.1 Context

The South-West Region of Cameroon has experienced significant social disruption since 2016 due to the ongoing Anglophone crisis. Displacement, economic hardship, and the breakdown of traditional community support structures have left many elderly widows without adequate care. In Buea, widows who have lost their husbands often face isolation, food insecurity, and deteriorating living conditions — particularly those too elderly or infirm to work.

### 2.2 Organizational Context

ADA was founded in 2021 by a group of young, faith-driven individuals in Cameroon who felt called to serve the most vulnerable members of their community. At the time of this project, the organization had no formal registration, no office, and no prior experience implementing community projects. The First Widow Home Visits project was conceived as both a direct act of service and a learning exercise — a way to understand the needs of the community firsthand before designing larger interventions.

### 2.3 Rationale

The project was motivated by:

- Reports from church communities about elderly widows living alone in difficult conditions
- A desire to move from intention to action — to officially begin ADA's mission
- The need to gather firsthand data on the living conditions of vulnerable widows in Buea
- A faith-based conviction that even small acts of compassion can have transformative impact

## 4. Activities Implemented

### 4.1 Preparation Phase (November 1–7)

The volunteer team spent the first week identifying potential beneficiaries through word-of-mouth referrals from local church communities and neighbors. This process proved more challenging than anticipated — many widows live in isolated locations without clear addresses, and community members were sometimes hesitant to share personal information about their neighbors.

The team pooled personal funds to purchase bags of rice, cooking oil, and basic condiments from the local market and organized transportation logistics for reaching the widows' homes.

### 4.2 Visit One: The Widow Living Alone

The first visit was to an elderly widow estimated to be in her late 70s, living alone in a small rented room in a compound house. The volunteers arrived with a bag of rice, introduced themselves and the purpose of their visit, and spent time listening to her story. She had lost her husband over a decade ago and had no children nearby to support her. The team prayed with her, documented her living conditions (basic but clean, limited food stores, no visible health concerns), and noted her primary need: companionship and regular food support.

### 4.3 Visit Two: The Widow with a Small Garden

The second widow, estimated to be in her early 70s, was found in a modest home with a small garden she tended for subsistence. Though in better physical condition than the first, she expressed deep loneliness and financial anxiety. She was unable to afford medical care for recurring joint pain. The volunteers delivered rice, prayed with her, and documented her need for basic healthcare access and economic support.

### 4.4 Visit Three: The Bedridden Widow in the Makeshift Shelter

The third visit was the most impactful and emotionally difficult for the team. The volunteers found an elderly widow — estimated to be in her 80s — bedridden in a makeshift shelter with walls partially covered by paper and cardboard to block wind and rain. Despite her condition, she was the primary caregiver for a young grandchild whose parents were absent. The shelter offered minimal protection from the elements, and the widow's health was visibly deteriorating.

The volunteers delivered rice, prayed with the widow and her grandchild, and spent extended time documenting the conditions. This visit profoundly affected the team and became a defining moment for ADA's mission — a concrete illustration of why the organization needed to exist.

### 4.5 Post-Visit Documentation (November 20–30)

Following the visits, the team compiled notes, discussed findings, and began planning next steps. Photographs were taken (with consent) to document conditions for future reference and fundraising purposes.

## 5. Beneficiary Profiles

All names have been withheld to protect the privacy and dignity of the beneficiaries.

### Beneficiary A — "The Quiet One"

- **Estimated age:** Late 70s
- **Living situation:** Single rented room in a compound house
- **Health status:** Mobile, no acute health concerns observed
- **Family support:** No children or relatives nearby
- **Primary needs:** Regular food supply, companionship, social connection
- **Notable observations:** Clean and organized living space despite limited resources; expressed gratitude for the visit and said no one had checked on her in months

### Beneficiary B — "The Gardener"

- **Estimated age:** Early 70s
- **Living situation:** Modest home with small subsistence garden
- **Health status:** Mobile but experiencing chronic joint pain
- **Family support:** Limited; some distant relatives but no regular contact
- **Primary needs:** Healthcare access, economic support, food supplementation
- **Notable observations:** Resourceful and independent but increasingly unable to maintain her garden; worried about what would happen when she could no longer tend it

### Beneficiary C — "The Grandmother"

- **Estimated age:** Early 80s
- **Living situation:** Makeshift shelter with paper-covered walls; bedridden
- **Health status:** Bedridden, visibly deteriorating health, no access to medical care
- **Family support:** Caring for one grandchild; parents of the child absent
- **Primary needs:** Urgent shelter repair or relocation, medical attention, childcare support, consistent food supply
- **Notable observations:** Despite her condition, her primary concern was for her grandchild's welfare rather than her own; the shelter was structurally unsafe and offered inadequate protection from weather

## 6. Outcomes & Impact

### 6.1 Immediate Outcomes

- **Food security (short-term):** Each widow received a food package (25kg rice, cooking oil, and condiments) sufficient for approximately 2–3 weeks of meals
- **Spiritual and emotional support:** All three widows expressed deep appreciation for the prayer and companionship; two stated it was the first visit they had received in months
- **Dignity and recognition:** The act of visiting communicated to each widow that she was seen, valued, and not forgotten by her community

### 6.2 Organizational Outcomes

- **ADA officially launched:** This project transformed ADA from an idea into an active organization
- **Needs assessment completed:** The team now had firsthand knowledge of the conditions facing elderly widows in Buea
- **Team formation:** The three volunteers solidified their commitment to ADA's mission through shared experience
- **Documentation created:** Photos and notes from the visits became the foundation for future fundraising and program design

### 6.3 Longer-Term Impact

- The findings from Visit Three (the bedridden widow in the makeshift shelter) became a catalyst for ADA's subsequent shelter and housing initiatives
- The emotional impact on volunteers strengthened their resolve and attracted additional volunteers in subsequent months
- The project established ADA's model of combining material assistance with spiritual care and needs documentation

## 7. Challenges & Lessons Learned

### 7.1 Challenges Encountered

| Challenge | Description | How It Was Addressed |
|---|---|---|
| **Locating beneficiaries** | No formal addresses; relied on verbal directions and community referrals | Spent additional time asking neighbors; arrived early to allow for searching |
| **Emotional impact on volunteers** | The conditions witnessed — particularly the bedridden widow — were emotionally overwhelming for the young team | Team debriefed together after each visit; leaned on faith and prayer for processing |
| **Limited resources** | $200 budget meant only basic food items could be provided; could not address shelter or medical needs | Documented needs for future projects; accepted the limitation of this first effort |
| **No organizational infrastructure** | No transport, no office, no formal processes | Used personal phones for documentation; walked or used public transport; coordinated via WhatsApp |
| **Community trust** | Some community members were initially suspicious of young strangers asking about vulnerable widows | Introduced themselves through church connections; explained ADA's mission clearly |

### 7.2 Lessons Learned

1. **Start where you are.** ADA did not wait for perfect conditions, formal registration, or large funding to begin serving. The act of starting — however small — was itself transformative for both the beneficiaries and the organization.

2. **Emotional preparation matters.** Young volunteers encountering extreme poverty and suffering for the first time need support structures. Future projects should include pre-visit briefings and post-visit debriefing sessions.

3. **Documentation is essential.** The notes and photos from these visits proved invaluable for future planning, fundraising, and organizational storytelling. Every project should prioritize documentation from day one.

4. **Food is necessary but not sufficient.** While the rice was appreciated, the widows' most pressing needs were often structural (shelter, healthcare, companionship). Future programming must address root causes, not just symptoms.

5. **Community entry requires relationships.** Finding and accessing vulnerable individuals requires trust and local connections. ADA should invest in building relationships with community leaders, churches, and local authorities.

6. **Small teams can accomplish meaningful work.** Three committed volunteers with $200 were able to launch an organization, serve three families, and generate insights that would shape years of future programming.

## 9. Recommendations for Future Projects

Based on the findings and lessons from this inaugural project, the team recommends the following:

### 9.1 Immediate Follow-Up

1. **Return visits:** Schedule monthly follow-up visits to all three widows to maintain relationship and monitor conditions
2. **Urgent intervention for Beneficiary C:** Her shelter situation requires immediate attention — explore options for repair, relocation, or community-supported rebuilding
3. **Medical referral:** Connect Beneficiary C with local health services or a community health worker

### 9.2 Program Development

4. **Expand beneficiary identification:** Develop a systematic process for identifying vulnerable widows through churches, community leaders, and local social services
5. **Diversify assistance:** Move beyond food-only support to include healthcare access, shelter improvement, and companionship programs
6. **Volunteer training:** Develop a brief orientation program for new volunteers covering emotional preparedness, documentation standards, and community engagement protocols

### 9.3 Organizational Growth

7. **Formal registration:** Pursue legal registration of ADA to enable formal partnerships and funding applications
8. **Fundraising strategy:** Use documentation from this project to develop fundraising materials for larger interventions
9. **Partnership development:** Connect with local churches, health facilities, and other NGOs for referral pathways and resource sharing
10. **Record-keeping systems:** Establish simple but consistent systems for tracking beneficiaries, visits, and outcomes over time

## 11. Conclusion

The First Widow Home Visits project was, by conventional metrics, a small intervention — three visits, three bags of rice, $200 spent. But its significance to the Apoti Development Association cannot be overstated. This project was the moment ADA moved from aspiration to action, from conversation to compassion in practice.

What the team found in those homes — particularly the image of an elderly, bedridden grandmother caring for a grandchild in a paper-walled shelter — became the moral foundation upon which ADA would build all of its subsequent work. It answered the question of whether the need was real, whether young people could make a difference, and whether faith could be translated into tangible service.

The three volunteers who walked those unpaved roads in November 2021 did not have experience, infrastructure, or significant resources. What they had was willingness — and that proved to be enough to begin. ADA's story starts here: not with a grand launch or a large grant, but with a bag of rice, a prayer, and the courage to knock on a stranger's door.

This report is submitted with gratitude to God and to the three widows who welcomed us into their homes and their lives.
