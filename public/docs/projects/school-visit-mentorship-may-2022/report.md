# Project Report: School Visit & Mentorship Program

| Field | Details |
|-------|---------|

| **Period** | May 1–31, 2022 |
| **Location** | Buea, South-West Region, Cameroon |
| **Beneficiaries Reached** | 25 secondary school students |
| **Budget Spent** | 165,000 CFA ($275 USD) of 180,000 CFA ($300 USD) |

## 1. Executive Summary

ADA conducted its first in-school mentorship visit in May 2022, engaging 25 secondary school students (ages 10–14, predominantly boys in blue uniforms) in Buea. The project marked a strategic shift from one-time distributions to relationship-based engagement within an existing school structure. All planned activities were successfully delivered under budget.

## 2. Context & Rationale

This project represented a pivotal change in ADA's approach:

- **Previous model:** One-time distributions organized independently by ADA
- **New model:** Working within an existing school structure during regular hours
- **Strategic shift:** From event-based to relationship-based engagement

This was the first time ADA embedded its activities within a school's daily routine rather than organizing a standalone event.

## 3. Activities Delivered

### 3.1 School Visit

The ADA team of 6 volunteers arrived at the school during regular hours. Students were in Chemistry class when the team arrived. The school administration welcomed ADA and facilitated the engagement.

### 3.2 Mentorship Session

- Delivered encouragement and motivational talks to 25 students
- Provided life skills guidance and academic motivation
- Distributed educational materials and handouts
- Created an interactive and supportive environment

### 3.3 Feeding Program

- Provided food and refreshments to all 25 students
- Snacks and drinks served during the mentorship session
- School contributed additional refreshments (reducing costs)

### 3.4 Relationship Building

- Established ongoing partnership with school administration
- Agreed on framework for future visits
- School expressed interest in continued collaboration

## 5. Key Observations

- Students were receptive and engaged throughout the session
- Working within school hours ensured full attendance
- School staff were supportive and collaborative
- The in-school model is more sustainable than standalone events
- Blue-uniformed students (predominantly boys, ages 10–14) were the primary beneficiaries

## 6. Lessons Learned

1. **Institutional partnerships work** — Embedding within school structures provides access, credibility, and sustainability
2. **Cost efficiency** — Schools can contribute resources (refreshments), reducing project costs
3. **Attendance guaranteed** — Visiting during school hours ensures beneficiary participation
4. **Relationship over transaction** — Ongoing engagement creates deeper impact than one-time distributions

## 7. Recommendations

1. Replicate the in-school model at additional schools in Buea
2. Develop a structured mentorship curriculum for repeat visits
3. Schedule quarterly follow-up visits to maintain the relationship
4. Document the model as a template for future school-based interventions
