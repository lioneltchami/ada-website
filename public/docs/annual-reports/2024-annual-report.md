## Message from the Founder

Dear friends, partners, and supporters,

2024 was a milestone year for ADA — our biggest, boldest, and most impactful since we began in 2021. What started three years ago as a single act of service with a $200 budget and 3 beneficiaries has grown into a multi-city, multi-program organization that delivered **$12,800 in programming** to **520+ beneficiaries** across **6 projects** in 2024.

This year, we achieved three firsts that mark ADA's evolution from a volunteer initiative into an organization with proven delivery capacity:

**Our first infrastructure project.** In Nkwen-Mbatu village, we drilled a borehole and installed a solar-powered pump that now provides clean water to over 300 people. The day water first flowed, the village chief declared a community holiday. At $14.72 per beneficiary with a 15-year projected lifespan, this is the kind of cost-effective, lasting impact we exist to create.

**Our first emergency relief response.** When the crisis in Bamenda displaced families from their homes, we mobilized within weeks — distributing food supplies to 50 IDP families over three months. We demonstrated that ADA can respond to urgent need, not only planned programming.

**Our largest single event.** Over 80 young women gathered for a two-day empowerment conference in Buea — our biggest gathering ever. The energy, the testimonies, and the connections formed confirmed that investing in young women creates ripple effects across entire communities.

We also ran back-to-school campaigns in two cities simultaneously for the first time, supporting 40 orphaned children in Buea and Bamenda. Our Christmas campaign grew to serve 50 children, up from 30 just two years ago.

The numbers tell the story: from $200 to $12,800 in budget. From 3 to 520 beneficiaries. From 1 project to 6. From 1 city to 3 locations. This trajectory is not accidental — it reflects disciplined growth, faithful stewardship, and a team that delivers.

As we look to 2025, we carry forward the confidence that comes from proven capacity. We have demonstrated we can build infrastructure, respond to emergencies, run multi-city operations, and convene large gatherings. The foundation is set for even greater impact.

Thank you for walking this journey with us.

**Lionel Tchami Nfada, MSc**

## Year in Review: Project Summaries

### 1. Emergency Food Relief — Bamenda Crisis (January–April)

When escalating conflict in the North-West Region displaced families from their homes, ADA mobilized its first-ever emergency relief response. Over three months, we distributed food supplies to 50 internally displaced families — representing over 150 individuals — in Bamenda. Packages included rice, oil, salt, beans, and other staples sufficient to sustain a family for two weeks per distribution cycle.

This project demonstrated ADA's capacity to respond rapidly to humanitarian need beyond our planned programming calendar. Working through our Bamenda office under Regional Coordinator Corrine Mbekenyui, we identified beneficiary families through community leaders and church networks, ensuring aid reached the most vulnerable. The project came in $83 under its $2,500 budget at a total cost of $2,417 (1,450,000 CFA).

### 2. Clean Water Well — Nkwen-Mbatu (June–October)

ADA's flagship 2024 achievement: our first infrastructure project. In partnership with local authorities and drilling contractors, we constructed a borehole fitted with a solar-powered pump in Nkwen-Mbatu village, providing clean water access to over 300 community members. The project spanned five months from site survey through completion and commissioning.

The day water first flowed from the well, the village chief declared a community holiday. Families gathered to celebrate — many had walked over a kilometer daily to fetch water from unreliable sources. At a total cost of $4,417 (2,650,000 CFA) and a per-beneficiary cost of just $14.72, with a projected 15-year lifespan, this well represents exceptional value. It proves ADA can deliver lasting infrastructure, not only consumable aid.

### 3. Back-to-School Bamenda (September)

For the first time, ADA ran simultaneous back-to-school campaigns in two cities. In Bamenda, we equipped 15 orphaned children with school supplies, uniforms, shoes, and registration fees for the 2024–2025 academic year. Each child received a complete package ensuring they could begin the school year with dignity and confidence.

Working through established relationships with orphanages and community caregivers, our Bamenda team identified children who would otherwise miss the start of school due to financial barriers. The project cost $1,183 (710,000 CFA) against a $1,200 budget — a testament to careful procurement and local knowledge.

### 4. Back-to-School Buea (September)

Running concurrently with the Bamenda campaign, our Buea back-to-school project served 25 orphaned children with comprehensive school packages. The larger cohort in Buea reflects our deeper community relationships in our headquarters city and the growing trust families place in ADA's consistent annual support.

At $1,575 (945,000 CFA) against a $1,600 budget, the Buea campaign maintained the same quality standards as Bamenda while serving a larger group. Combined, the two campaigns supported 40 children — a significant expansion from previous single-city efforts and a model we intend to replicate and grow.

### 5. Young Women Empowerment Conference (October 27–28)

Our largest single event in ADA's history brought together over 80 young women for a two-day conference in Buea. The program featured sessions on financial literacy, leadership development, health and wellness, spiritual growth, and entrepreneurship. Guest speakers included successful women professionals, pastors, and community leaders.

The conference created a space for young women to envision their futures, build networks, and access practical knowledge often unavailable to them. Feedback was overwhelmingly positive, with participants requesting follow-up mentorship programs — a request we are incorporating into our 2025 planning. The event cost $1,967 (1,180,000 CFA) against a $2,000 budget.

### 6. Christmas Giving 2024 (December 18)

Our annual Christmas campaign grew to serve 50 orphaned children in Buea — up from 30 in 2022. Each child received gifts, new clothing, food packages, and participated in a celebration event with games, music, and a shared meal. For many, this is the only Christmas gift they receive.

The campaign cost $983 (590,000 CFA) against a $1,000 budget. Beyond the material gifts, the event provides these children with a sense of belonging and celebration during a season that can otherwise highlight their loss. Community volunteers and church partners contributed time and venue support, multiplying our investment.

## Infrastructure Milestone: The Clean Water Well

### ADA's Flagship 2024 Achievement

The Nkwen-Mbatu Clean Water Well represents a transformational moment for ADA. For the first time, we moved beyond consumable aid into **permanent infrastructure** — creating an asset that will serve a community for 15+ years.

### Project Specifications

| Specification | Detail |
|---------------|--------|
| Type | Borehole with solar-powered pump |
| Location | Nkwen-Mbatu village, North-West Region |
| Timeline | June–October 2024 (5 months) |
| Total Cost | $4,417 (2,650,000 CFA) |
| Beneficiaries | 300+ community members |
| Cost per Beneficiary | $14.72 |
| Projected Lifespan | 15 years |
| Cost per Beneficiary per Year | $0.98 |

### The Journey

**June 2024:** Site survey and community consultation. Village leaders identified the optimal location based on geological assessment and community access patterns.

**July–August 2024:** Drilling and construction. Professional contractors drilled the borehole to reach the water table, then installed casing and a solar-powered pump system — eliminating ongoing fuel costs and ensuring sustainable operation.

**September 2024:** Testing and commissioning. Water quality testing confirmed safe drinking water standards. The pump system was calibrated for optimal flow.

**October 2024:** Community handover and celebration. The village chief declared a holiday when water first flowed. Families gathered to celebrate the end of long daily walks to distant, unreliable water sources.

### Why This Matters

| Before | After |
|--------|-------|
| 1+ km walk to water source | Water point in the village |
| Unreliable seasonal sources | Year-round clean water |
| Waterborne illness risk | Safe drinking water |
| Hours lost daily (women/children) | Time freed for school/work |
| No infrastructure investment | Proven delivery capacity |

### Organizational Significance

This project proves ADA can:
- **Plan and execute** multi-month infrastructure projects
- **Manage contractors** and technical specifications
- **Deliver lasting assets** — not only consumable aid
- **Achieve exceptional cost efficiency** — under $15 per beneficiary for 15 years of service
- **Engage communities** as partners, not only recipients

The clean water well establishes the template for future infrastructure projects and demonstrates to funders that ADA has the capacity for capital-intensive, high-impact programming.

## Challenges & Lessons Learned

### Challenges Faced

**1. Security Constraints (North-West Region)**
The ongoing crisis in the NW Region created logistical challenges for our Bamenda operations. Movement restrictions, communication disruptions, and community displacement required adaptive planning and flexible timelines.

**2. Currency Fluctuation**
Operating with USD-denominated budgets while spending in CFA francs exposed programs to exchange rate variability. The 2024 rate averaged approximately 600 CFA per dollar, requiring careful financial management.

**3. Scaling Operations Across Cities**
Running simultaneous programs in Buea and Bamenda for the first time stretched coordination capacity. Communication between offices and synchronized reporting required new systems.

**4. Infrastructure Project Complexity**
The clean water well — our first infrastructure project — introduced technical requirements (geological surveys, contractor management, quality testing) beyond our previous experience with direct aid distribution.

### Lessons Learned

**1. Local leadership is essential.** The Bamenda emergency response succeeded because Corrine and her team had pre-existing community relationships. Invest in local leaders before crises occur.

**2. Infrastructure requires patience.** The 5-month well timeline taught us that lasting assets cannot be rushed. Build realistic timelines into infrastructure proposals.

**3. Multi-city operations need systems.** Simultaneous back-to-school campaigns revealed the need for standardized reporting templates, shared procurement processes, and regular inter-office communication rhythms.

**4. Community ownership ensures sustainability.** The Nkwen-Mbatu community's celebration and the chief's involvement signal genuine ownership — the best predictor of long-term infrastructure maintenance.

**5. Budget discipline builds trust.** Finishing 2.0% under budget across all six projects demonstrates the financial stewardship that funders and partners require.

## Looking Ahead: 2025 Plans

### Strategic Priorities

Building on 2024's breakthroughs, ADA's 2025 programming focuses on four pillars:

### 1. Maternal & Prenatal Healthcare

**Partnership with CMA Hospital** — Leveraging the relationship established in 2024, we will launch a prenatal care program supporting vulnerable pregnant women with:
- Antenatal care visits and monitoring
- Nutritional support during pregnancy
- Safe delivery assistance
- Postnatal follow-up

This represents ADA's entry into healthcare programming — another capability expansion.

### 2. Youth Mentorship Program

Responding directly to feedback from the 2024 Women's Conference, we will establish a structured mentorship program pairing young women with professional mentors for:
- Career guidance and skill development
- Financial literacy and savings groups
- Leadership development
- Ongoing community and accountability

### 3. Community Development

- **Continued community cleanups** — Environmental stewardship programs
- **Infrastructure maintenance** — Ensuring the Nkwen-Mbatu well remains operational
- **Potential second water project** — Site assessment for additional community infrastructure

### 4. Widow Support — Cohort 3

Building on our established widow empowerment model, Cohort 3 will provide:
- Skills training and income-generation support
- Community and peer support networks
- Material assistance for the most vulnerable

### 2025 Budget Target

Based on our growth trajectory and expanded programming, ADA targets a 2025 budget of **$18,000–$22,000** — representing continued disciplined growth while maintaining our zero-overhead model.
