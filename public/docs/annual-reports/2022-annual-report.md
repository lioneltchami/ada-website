## Message from the Founder

Dear friends, partners, and supporters,

When we registered ADA in November 2021, we were three volunteers visiting widows in their homes with food baskets and prayer. We had no office, no budget, and no formal programs — just a conviction that God was calling us to serve the most vulnerable in our communities.

Twelve months later, I am humbled to present this report documenting what God accomplished through this small team in 2022: **seven projects delivered, 140 lives touched, expansion to a second city, and the launch of our first vocational training program.**

The growth has been extraordinary. From three widows receiving home visits, we scaled to organized programs serving widows, orphans, internally displaced girls, and the broader community. Our team grew from 3 to 8+ dedicated volunteers. We opened operations in Bamenda alongside our Buea headquarters. We delivered a project nearly every month.

But the numbers only tell part of the story. Behind each statistic is a widow who learned tailoring and now feeds her children through her own labor. A displaced teenage girl who received baking training and a startup kit to build a small business. An orphan who returned to school with supplies and the knowledge that someone believes in their future.

We operated with discipline — spending $7,442 against a $7,700 budget, delivering 3.3% under budget while reaching every beneficiary we targeted. This fiscal responsibility reflects our commitment to honoring every franc entrusted to us.

2022 proved that ADA's model works: faith-driven service, community-rooted programs, and disciplined execution. We enter 2023 with momentum, experience, and a clear vision for scaling our impact.

Thank you for believing in this mission before there was evidence. The evidence is now here.

In service,

**Lionel Tchami Nfada, MSc**
Founder & President
Apoti Development Association

## About ADA

### Who We Are

Apoti Development Association (ADA) is a faith-based non-profit organization empowering vulnerable communities in Cameroon. Founded in November 2021 and legally registered under **N° 415/G.37/D14/VolI/SAAJP**, ADA operates from its headquarters in Buea, South-West Region, with a second office in Bamenda, North-West Region.

To shine the light of Jesus Christ by empowering vulnerable communities — widows, orphans, and young women — through sustainable programs that restore dignity and create opportunity.

## Year in Review — Project Summaries

### 1. School Supply Distribution — February 2022

**Location:** Buea | **Beneficiaries:** 20 orphans | **Actual Cost:** $467 (280,000 CFA)

ADA's first project of 2022 provided school supplies to 20 orphans in Buea at the start of the academic term. Each child received exercise books, pens, pencils, mathematical sets, and school bags. This intervention removed a critical barrier to education for children whose guardians could not afford basic supplies. The project also established ADA's relationship with local schools and orphan caregivers that would support later programs.

### 2. Widow Vocational Training — Cohort 1 (March–September 2022)

**Location:** Bamenda | **Beneficiaries:** 15 widows | **Actual Cost:** $2,917 (1,750,000 CFA)

Our flagship program of 2022 and ADA's most ambitious undertaking to date. Fifteen widows in Bamenda enrolled in a six-month tailoring and dressmaking training program. Participants received sewing machines, fabric, and materials alongside structured instruction. By September, **12 of 15 participants (80%) graduated and began earning income** through their new skills. This program validated ADA's theory of change: that sustainable empowerment comes through skills, not just aid. It also marked our geographic expansion to Bamenda.

### 3. Widow Support — Food & Financial Aid (March 2022)

**Location:** Buea | **Beneficiaries:** 15 widows | **Actual Cost:** $767 (460,000 CFA)

Concurrent with the vocational training launch, ADA provided immediate relief to 15 widows in Buea through food packages and direct financial assistance. This program addressed urgent needs — nutrition and household expenses — while our longer-term vocational programs were being established. Each widow received a food basket and a financial grant to cover pressing obligations such as rent, medical bills, or children's school fees.

### 4. School Visit & Mentorship — May 2022

**Location:** Buea | **Beneficiaries:** 25 orphans | **Actual Cost:** $275 (165,000 CFA)

ADA volunteers visited schools in Buea to deliver mentorship sessions to 25 orphans. Beyond material support, this program addressed the emotional and developmental needs of children growing up without parents. Volunteers shared life skills, encouraged academic persistence, and provided a caring adult presence. This low-cost, high-impact model demonstrated that meaningful intervention doesn't always require large budgets.

### 5. IDP Girls Baking Training & Startup Kits — July 2022

**Location:** Buea | **Beneficiaries:** 15 IDP girls | **Actual Cost:** $1,458 (875,000 CFA)

ADA's first program targeting internally displaced persons (IDPs) — young women displaced by the ongoing Anglophone crisis. Fifteen girls received intensive baking training followed by startup kits containing ovens, baking supplies, and initial ingredients to launch small businesses. This program recognized that displaced young women face unique vulnerabilities and need economic empowerment to rebuild their lives. The startup kit model ensured participants could immediately apply their training.

### 6. Back-to-School 2022 — September 2022

**Location:** Buea | **Beneficiaries:** 20 orphans | **Actual Cost:** $975 (585,000 CFA)

As the new academic year began, ADA provided comprehensive back-to-school packages to 20 orphans. Building on our February distribution, this program included uniforms, shoes, and school fees in addition to supplies — a more holistic intervention addressing the full cost of education. This ensured children could start the year on equal footing with their peers.

### 7. Christmas Giving 2022 — December 2022

**Location:** Buea | **Beneficiaries:** 30 (widows & orphans) | **Actual Cost:** $583 (350,000 CFA)

ADA closed 2022 with our inaugural Christmas campaign, bringing holiday joy to 30 widows and orphans in Buea. Beneficiaries received food packages, clothing, and gifts. Beyond material provision, the event created community — bringing together people who often feel forgotten during the holiday season. This campaign established a tradition we intend to grow annually.

### Overhead Analysis

ADA operated with **zero administrative overhead** in 2022. All funds went directly to program delivery. This was possible because:

- All team members served as unpaid volunteers
- No office rent was incurred (home-based operations)
- Communication and coordination costs were absorbed personally by leadership
- Transportation to project sites was donated or personally funded

**100% of donor funds went directly to beneficiaries.**

### Fiscal Discipline

Every project came in under budget, with total savings of $258 (3.3%). This reflects careful procurement, negotiated rates with local suppliers, and a culture of stewardship. Savings were retained for future programming.

## Challenges & Lessons Learned

### Challenges

**1. Operating in a Crisis Context**

The ongoing Anglophone crisis in Cameroon's North-West and South-West regions created security challenges, displaced populations, and economic instability. Program delivery required flexibility and security awareness.

**2. Limited Funding Pipeline**

As a new organization, ADA relied primarily on personal networks and small individual donations. We lacked institutional funding relationships, limiting our ability to plan multi-year programs.

**3. Volunteer Retention & Capacity**

While our team grew, managing volunteers without compensation presented challenges. Competing demands on volunteers' time occasionally affected project timelines.

**4. Monitoring & Evaluation**

With limited resources, our M&E systems were basic. We tracked outputs (beneficiaries reached, items distributed) but lacked robust tools for measuring long-term outcomes.

### Lessons Learned

**1. Vocational training delivers lasting impact.** The 80% graduation rate and income generation from our tailoring cohort confirmed that skills-based programs create sustainable change — not dependency.

**2. Start small, prove the model, then scale.** Each project built credibility and capacity for the next. Our February school supply distribution (20 beneficiaries, $467) gave us confidence to attempt the six-month vocational program ($2,917).

**3. Geographic expansion requires local leadership.** Our Bamenda operations succeeded because we had a trusted local team, not because we managed remotely from Buea.

**4. Fiscal discipline builds trust.** Coming in 3.3% under budget across all projects demonstrates stewardship that funders and partners can rely on.

**5. Diverse programming reduces risk.** Serving four demographics across multiple program types meant that challenges in one area didn't halt all operations.
